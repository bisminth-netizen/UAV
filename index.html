UAV Flight Planning & GSD
L2 · IMPLEMENTATION DESIGN · HIGH CALCULATION PROBABILITY
VERY LIKELY CALCULATION QUESTION The professor demonstrated a worked example with a Sony α7R-III camera. You may be given a camera spec and asked to calculate (1) flight altitude for a target map scale, or (2) GSD. Know these formulas cold.
SCALE FORMULA
Scale = f / H   →   1 : M = f / H
f = focal length (mm) · H = flight altitude (m) · M = scale denominator
To get H:   H = f × M   (ensure consistent units!)
GROUND SAMPLING DISTANCE (GSD)
GSD = (H × pixel_size) / focal_length
H = flight altitude · pixel_size = physical size of one sensor pixel (mm) · focal_length (mm)
Or equivalently: GSD = pixel_size × (H / f) = pixel_size × scale_denominator
⚠ Units must be consistent — H must be converted to mm (e.g. 175 m → 175,000 mm)
WORKED EXAMPLE — LIKELY EXAM TYPE Camera: Sony α7R-III. Sensor: 35.9 mm × 24.0 mm. Resolution: 7952 × 5304 px. Focal length: 35 mm. Target scale: 1:5,000.

Step 1 — Pixel size: 35.9 mm / 7952 px ≈ 0.00452 mm/px
Step 2 — Flight height: H = f × M = 35 mm × 5000 = 175,000 mm = 175 m
Step 3 — GSD: GSD = 0.00452 × 5000 = 22.6 mm/px = 2.26 cm/px
Image Overlap — Why It Matters
Front overlap + side overlap for photogrammetry
ESSAYMCQ
▼

For 3D reconstruction and seamless mosaics, images must overlap. Two key parameters:

  • Front Overlap (Endlap) — overlap between images along the flight direction
  • Side Overlap (Sidelap) — overlap between adjacent flight strips
WHY OVERLAP? Overlapping images allow the same 3D point to appear in multiple photos from different angles. This enables feature matching (SIFT), triangulation, and sparse/dense point cloud generation.

Aerial Triangulation (AT) & Bundle Adjustment (BA)

  • AT — estimating 3D locations using aerial images + camera parameters
  • Bundle Adjustment (BA) — Least Squares optimization that simultaneously adjusts camera poses and 3D point positions. Each 3D point sends a "bundle of rays" to multiple cameras. Goal: minimize the sum of squared reprojection errors: min Σ ||x_obs − x_pred||²
BUNDLE ADJUSTMENT INSIGHT BA is an iterative Least Squares solver. It jointly refines: (1) camera exterior orientation (Xs,Ys,Zs,ω,φ,κ), (2) camera interior orientation (f,x₀,y₀), and (3) 3D GCP/tie-point coordinates — all at once. Reprojection error = distance (pixels) between observed and re-projected image point.

Ground Control Points (GCPs)
Georeferencing and accuracy
ESSAY
▼

GCPs are points on the ground visible in drone photos and measured in a real-world coordinate system (using RTK/PPK GPS — NOT a phone!).

  • Good spatial distribution is critical — spread across the whole area, not clustered
  • More points ≠ better unless well-distributed
  • Correct geometric distortions in the model
  • Enable accurate georeferencing to real-world coordinates
COMMON MISTAKE TO AVOID IN THE EXAM Never say "more GCPs always means better accuracy." The distribution matters far more than the quantity.
GCP Accuracy — Residual Error & RMSE
How to quantify georeferencing accuracy
CALC
▼

After bundle adjustment, each GCP has a residual — the difference between observed (measured in field) and computed (model output) position.

GCP RESIDUAL & RMSE
Residual = Observed_coord − Computed_coord
RMSE_X = √( Σ ΔXᵢ² / n )
RMSE_Y = √( Σ ΔYᵢ² / n )
RMSE_Z = √( Σ ΔZᵢ² / n )
RMSE_XY = √( RMSE_X² + RMSE_Y² )  [horizontal accuracy]
n = number of GCPs · ΔXᵢ, ΔYᵢ, ΔZᵢ = residuals at GCP i
WORKED EXAMPLE 4 GCPs with residuals (metres): GCP1(ΔX=0.02, ΔY=0.03, ΔZ=0.04), GCP2(ΔX=−0.01, ΔY=0.02, ΔZ=−0.03), GCP3(ΔX=0.03, ΔY=−0.01, ΔZ=0.02), GCP4(ΔX=−0.02, ΔY=0.01, ΔZ=−0.01)

RMSE_X = √((0.02²+0.01²+0.03²+0.02²)/4) = √(0.00045) ≈ 0.021 m
RMSE_Y = √((0.03²+0.02²+0.01²+0.01²)/4) = √(0.000375) ≈ 0.019 m
RMSE_XY = √(0.021²+0.019²) ≈ 0.028 m = 2.8 cm (horizontal)
RMSE_Z = √((0.04²+0.03²+0.02²+0.01²)/4) ≈ 0.027 m = 2.7 cm (vertical)

Projection & Coordinate Systems
L3 · PHOTOGRAMMETRY FUNDAMENTALS · MCQ + ESSAY
Central (Perspective) Projection

Light rays from an object pass through a single viewpoint (perspective center S), then form an image on a plane.

  • Creates natural images with perspective distortion
  • Objects farther away appear smaller
  • This is how regular cameras work
KEY CONCEPT The perspective center S, image point a, and ground point A are always collinear. This is the foundation of the collinearity equation.
Parallel Projection

Light rays are parallel to each other, perpendicular or at an angle to the projection plane.

  • No vanishing point — objects don't shrink with distance
  • Eliminates perspective distortion
  • Two types: orthogonal (90°) and oblique

Example: orthophoto (orthorectified image) uses parallel projection

Coordinate Systems — Three You Must Know

1. Image Space Coordinate System (S-xyz)
ESSAY
▼
  • S = perspective center (optical center of lens) — origin
  • x, y = parallel to image axes
  • z = principal optical axis (the direction the camera points)
EXAM QUESTION ALERT "Is the principal optical axis always vertical?" → NO. When the UAV tilts (roll, pitch, yaw), the optical axis tilts too. Only for perfectly nadir-pointing cameras is it truly vertical.
2. Interior Orientation Elements (f, x₀, y₀)
ESSAYMCQ
▼

Describe the camera's internal geometry — how a 3D point projects onto the 2D sensor:

  • f — Focal length: distance from lens to image plane where light converges
  • x₀ — Principal point offset in x from image center
  • y₀ — Principal point offset in y from image center

These are determined by camera calibration and stay fixed for a given camera.

3. Exterior Orientation Elements (Xs, Ys, Zs, ω, φ, κ)
ESSAYMCQ
▼

Describe where the camera is and how it's oriented in the real world — 3 translations + 3 rotations = 6 parameters:

ParameterMeaningProvided by
Xs, Ys, ZsCamera position in 3D spaceGPS (DGPS)
ω (omega)Roll — rotation about x-axisIMU
φ (phi)Pitch — rotation about y-axisIMU
κ (kappa)Yaw — rotation about z-axisIMU
REMEMBER The rotation follows the Right-Hand Screw Rule. ω = roll (x-axis), φ = pitch (y-axis), κ = yaw (z-axis).

Feature Extraction & Matching

★★ Harris Corner Detector — Calculation Ready
Given a pixel window, classify the region type
CALC★★★
▼

Harris detects corners by measuring how much the image changes when you shift a window in any direction.

Flat
λ₁ ≈ λ₂ ≈ small
No change in any direction
Edge
λ₁ >> λ₂ or λ₂ >> λ₁
Change in one direction
Corner
λ₁ ≈ λ₂ ≈ large
Change in ALL directions
STRUCTURE TENSOR (M)
M = Σ [ Ix²   IxIy ; IxIy   Iy² ]
Ix = horizontal gradient (Sobel X) · Iy = vertical gradient (Sobel Y)
Sobel X = [[-1,0,1],[-2,0,2],[-1,0,1]]   Sobel Y = [[-1,-2,-1],[0,0,0],[1,2,1]]
EIGENVALUE CALCULATION
det(M − λI) = 0 → find λ₁ and λ₂
For M = [[a,b],[c,d]]:   λ² − (a+d)λ + (ad−bc) = 0
trace = a+d · determinant = ad−bc
WORKED EXAMPLE (FROM LECTURE) Image patch all 10s → Ix = 120, Iy = 120 → M = [[14400, 14400],[14400, 14400]]
det(M − λI) = 0 → λ² − 28800λ = 0 → λ₁ = 28800, λ₂ = 0
Since λ₁ >> λ₂, this is an EDGE.
EXAM SURPRISE — UNIFORM IMAGE If all pixels have the same value, Ix = 0 and Iy = 0 everywhere → M = zero matrix → λ₁ = λ₂ = 0 → FLAT region. Be ready for this trick question!
★★ LoG Detector — Laplacian of Gaussian
CALCMCQ
▼

LoG = Gaussian blur (noise reduction) + Laplacian (second derivative). Used for blob detection.

LAPLACIAN (4-NEIGHBOR KERNEL)
K = [[0,1,0],[1,−4,1],[0,1,0]]
8-neighbor variant: K = [[1,1,1],[1,−8,1],[1,1,1]]
Apply via convolution: (I * K)(x,y) = ΣΣ I_ij × K_ij
  • L < 0 → bright blob (light region surrounded by dark)
  • L > 0 → dark blob (dark region surrounded by light)
WORKED EXAMPLE (FROM LECTURE) A bright center patch with surrounding pixels → Convolution result = −200 → L < 0 → Light Blob. Know the sign rule!
DoG — Difference of Gaussian
MCQESSAY
▼

DoG approximates LoG but is faster and more robust to noise. Used in SIFT.

DoG FORMULA
DoG(x, y, σ) = L(x, y, kσ) − L(x, y, σ)
L(x,y,σ) = G(x,y,σ) * I(x,y) — Gaussian-blurred image at scale σ
Measures: "how much does the image change when blur scale increases?"
AspectLoGDoG
OperationSecond derivativeSubtraction of blurred images
SpeedSlowerFaster
NoiseMore sensitiveMore robust
Used in SIFTTheory onlyPractical implementation
★★★ SIFT Algorithm — 6 Steps
Scale Invariant Feature Transform — most important feature descriptor
ESSAY★★★
▼

SIFT is invariant to scale, rotation, and partially to illumination changes. Produces a 128-dimensional descriptor for each keypoint.

Step 1 — Scale-Space Construction
Build a pyramid of DoG images at multiple scales (σ values)
Step 2 — Keypoint Detection
Find local extrema (maxima/minima) in DoG scale space — pixels extreme compared to all 26 neighbors
Step 3 — Keypoint Refinement
Remove low-contrast points and edge responses. Keep only stable, unique keypoints.
Step 4 — Orientation Assignment
Build 36-bin orientation histogram (360°). Dominant orientation(s) assigned. θ = arctan(Iy/Ix). Peaks ≥ 80% of max create additional keypoints.
Step 5 — Feature Descriptor Construction
16×16 window → 4×4 sub-blocks → each sub-block gets 8-bin histogram → 16 × 8 = 128 numbers. Gaussian weighting. Threshold at 0.2 for illumination invariance.
Step 6 — Feature Matching
Use Euclidean distance between 128-dim vectors. Threshold: too high = false positives, too low = false negatives.
WHY 128 DIMENSIONS? 16×16 window → 4×4 blocks → each block has 8-bin histogram → 16 blocks × 8 bins = 128.
ILLUMINATION INVARIANCE TRICK Any of the 128 values greater than 0.2 is clamped to 0.2, then re-normalized. This prevents strong gradients from dominating.

3D Reconstruction — Collinearity Equation
L5 · PHOTOGRAMMETRIC RECONSTRUCTION · HIGH CALCULATION PROBABILITY
THIS IS THE MATHEMATICAL HEART OF THE COURSE The collinearity equation connects: camera position (Xs,Ys,Zs), rotation (ω,φ,κ → matrix R), focal length (f,x₀,y₀), and ground coordinates (XA,YA,ZA). Understand the concept deeply.
COLLINEARITY EQUATION
x − x₀ = −f · [a₁(XA−Xs) + b₁(YA−Ys) + c₁(ZA−Zs)] / [a₃(XA−Xs) + b₃(YA−Ys) + c₃(ZA−Zs)]
y − y₀ = −f · [a₂(XA−Xs) + b₂(YA−Ys) + c₂(ZA−Zs)] / [a₃(XA−Xs) + b₃(YA−Ys) + c₃(ZA−Zs)]
x, y = image coordinates · x₀, y₀, f = interior orientation (calibration)
Xs, Ys, Zs = camera position (GPS) · XA, YA, ZA = ground point coordinates
a₁..c₃ = rotation matrix R elements (from ω, φ, κ via IMU)
THE "1 LINE, 3 POINTS" RULE Perspective center S, image point a, and ground point A are always on the same straight line. This one geometric fact generates both collinearity equations.
★★★ Backward Intersection — "What was my camera doing?"
ESSAYCALC★★★
▼

Known: Ground control points (GCPs) with known XA,YA,ZA AND their image coordinates x,y

Unknown: The 6 exterior orientation elements (Xs, Ys, Zs, ω, φ, κ)

Each GCP gives 2 equations (x and y). Need ≥ 3 non-collinear GCPs → 6 equations → solve 6 unknowns.

Applications in UAV

  • Determining camera position and orientation during flight
  • Georeferencing aerial images to real-world coordinates
  • Improving accuracy of 3D models and orthophotos
★★★ Forward Intersection — "Where is that point on the ground?"
ESSAYCALC★★★
▼

Known: Camera positions/orientations (GPS+IMU), interior orientation, image coordinates x,y from ≥ 2 stereo images

Unknown: Ground coordinates XA, YA, ZA

Two images give 4 equations → solve for 3 unknowns → over-determined, use Least Squares.

Structure from Motion (SfM) — Complete Pipeline
ESSAY
▼

SfM reconstructs 3D structure from 2D images by simultaneously estimating camera positions AND generating a 3D model.

Step 1: Feature detection & matching (SIFT)
Find keypoints in overlapping images, create 128-dim descriptors, match across images
Step 2: Camera pose estimation (Backward intersection)
Solve for exterior orientation parameters using matched keypoints
Step 3: Triangulation → Sparse point cloud
Use collinearity equation (forward intersection) to compute 3D position of each matched keypoint
Step 4: Bundle Adjustment
Least Squares optimization — simultaneously refine camera poses + 3D point positions. Minimizes sum of squared reprojection errors.
Step 5: Multi-View Stereo (MVS) → Dense point cloud
Use all images to fill in the gaps — generates millions of points instead of just keypoints
SPARSE vs DENSE POINT CLOUD Sparse = only keypoints (SIFT features). Dense = full surface via MVS. Dense has orders of magnitude more points.

LiDAR — Light Detection & Ranging
L6 · ACTIVE REMOTE SENSING · CALCULATION + ESSAY ★★★
DEFINITION LiDAR is an active remote sensing technology that measures distance with reflected laser light. First developed in 1960 by Hughes Aircraft Inc. Modern use enabled by DGPS and fast computers.
TIME-OF-FLIGHT (ToF) FORMULA — THE CORE OF LiDAR
Distance = (c × T) / 2
c = speed of light ≈ 3 × 10⁸ m/s · T = round-trip travel time
Divide by 2 because light travels TO the target AND BACK
WORKED EXAMPLE T = 2 × 10⁻⁶ s (2 microseconds)
Distance = (3×10⁸ × 2×10⁻⁶) / 2 = 600 / 2 = 300 m
3D POINT COORDINATES FROM LiDAR
X = Xs − r × cos(φ) × cos(θ)
Y = Ys − r × cos(φ) × sin(θ)
Z = Zs − r × sin(φ)
r = range distance · φ = elevation angle · θ = azimuth angle · (Xs,Ys,Zs) = sensor position from GPS/IMU
WORKED EXAMPLE Sensor at (Xs=0, Ys=0, Zs=100 m), range r=141.4 m, elevation φ=45°, azimuth θ=0°
X = 0 − 141.4 × cos(45°) × cos(0°) = −141.4 × 0.707 × 1 = −100 m
Y = 0 − 141.4 × cos(45°) × sin(0°) = 0 m
Z = 100 − 141.4 × sin(45°) = 100 − 100 = 0 m (ground level, 100 m horizontal from nadir)
★★★ LiDAR System Components
ESSAYMCQ★★★
▼
GPS (GNSS)

Provides precise position (Xs, Ys, Zs). Needs differential GPS (DGPS) for accuracy. Receives signals from multiple satellites.

IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit)

Provides orientation (ω, φ, κ) — roll, pitch, yaw. Essential because the aircraft is never perfectly level.

Laser Scanner

Emits laser pulses and captures returns. Records: Time-of-Flight, intensity, return number, timestamp for each reflected pulse.

★★★ Pulse Characteristics
MCQESSAY★★★
▼

1. Common Wavelengths

  • NIR (905 nm, 1064 nm) — most airborne and terrestrial LiDAR
  • SWIR (1550 nm) — high-power LiDAR applications
  • Blue-Green (~532 nm) — bathymetric LiDAR, penetrates water

2. Pulse Rate

  • Low-end: 50–100 kHz · Mid-range: 100–500 kHz · High-end: 500 kHz–1 MHz · Ultra: up to 2 MHz
  • Higher pulse rate → more points → higher density

3. Pulse Duration

  • Modern systems: 5–10 nanoseconds (ns)
  • Shorter → sharper return → more precise distance
  • Longer → more energy → better signal-to-noise for weak targets

4. Beam Divergence

BEAM DIVERGENCE
θ = D / L
θ = divergence angle (radians) · D = beam diameter at target · L = distance from source
WORKED EXAMPLE θ = 0.3 mrad = 0.0003 rad, flight altitude L = 500 m
Beam diameter: D = θ × L = 0.0003 × 500 = 0.15 m = 15 cm
→ Each LiDAR pulse footprint on the ground is a ~15 cm circle
★★ Point Density — Formula & Factors
CALCMCQ
▼
POINT DENSITY
D = N / A
D = point density (pts/m²) · N = total LiDAR points · A = area covered (m²)
WORKED EXAMPLE Pulse rate = 200,000 pulses/s, flight speed = 50 m/s, scan width = 200 m
Area swept/s = 50 × 200 = 10,000 m²/s
Point density = 200,000 / 10,000 = 20 pts/m²

Factors Affecting Point Density

  • Pulse Rate ↑ → Density ↑
  • Scan Angle ↑ → Density ↓ at edges
  • Flight Altitude ↑ → Density ↓
  • Flight Speed ↑ → Density ↓

DSM vs DTM vs CHM — Surface Model Definitions
Critical distinction for LiDAR data products
ESSAYMCQ
▼
ModelFull NameWhat it representsLiDAR Source
DSM Digital Surface Model ALL objects above ground — buildings, tree canopy, vehicles, power lines. The "first return" surface. First returns
DTM Digital Terrain Model Bare earth ONLY — ground points after removing all vegetation and structures via ground classification. Last/ground-classified returns

CHM FORMULA
CHM = DSM − DTM
Result = vegetation height above ground at each pixel location
e.g. CHM value of 15 m → tree canopy is 15 m above ground at that point
APPLICATION EXAMPLE Forest inventory: DSM shows top of canopy → DTM shows bare earth → CHM = DSM − DTM gives tree heights → used to estimate Above Ground Biomass (AGB).
